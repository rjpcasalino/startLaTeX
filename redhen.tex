\section*{}

Red Hen looked down at the desk contemplating that had he been armed he'd have unloaded a few rounds into his head but he knew it was pointless and he began to wonder. Why he was programmed at all to allow such thoughts and that thought looped for awhile and meantime he's looking like Martin Riggs staring at the face of his cocked pistol and feeling like a pustule.

The desk was assaulted and battered, chuck berry red blood pooling at the carpet it stood on. Why was he here again? He'd thought he'd like to know and even though it was equipped with NPUs and CPUs and TPUs and more RAM than\dots God knows---it still never really understood why it acted the way a human might and mostly forgot why it was doing what it was doing and for what reason and it was suppose to work that way. We flushed the cache in his head and tried again.

Red Hen had been told that nostalgia was fascist thinking. He finally found the physical media he had been sent for. He reached his right hand toward the murdered desk and extracted a fax and held it between both hands, his fingers giving us wonderful haptic feedback. It had all the names and accommodations of each VIP to the upcoming Peace Conference at Hartford, CT. Everything Red Hen looked at went into its hypercomputer and everything was sent back to Network.

Red Hen was constantly hooked into Network's feed. It played out before his ``eyes'' (two flat, rectangular steel plates; the eyes of its ``sensor head'')\footnote{Red Hen was designed by United States Heavy Industries and Research. Basically OmniCorp Consumer Products so it was little wonder the design team had an affinity for ``Robocane or RoboCop 2'' from the film \textit{RoboCop 2}. Red Hen's body was made to resemble the B1-Battle Droid from the Disney franchise we don't name albeit made to look even more human. This was so that it could wear human gear and put Advisors more at ease. It left things to be desired but Red Hen was better off looking this way than the minigun equipped agile walking-tank the designers originally had in mind.} that reflected his mind to itself the news that mattered to him or was suppose to matter to him: Operation Just Cause III; little resistance in Northern Mexico. A besieged city-state is what they were calling Mexico City. Who would have guessed that ``McNamara's Boys'' (Red Hen's unit) were embedded with Mexican nationals and at the tone\dots

Red Hen started across Red Mountain toward McNamara Hut. Extract was there and his unit wanted to take a stand and raze the absurd and ahistorical museum the Party had made out of the Hut. Snow kept getting deeper and we remote operators were well aware of all the warnings piling up in the associated log files but we weren't concerned because Red Hen had been designed for this and everything else. Plus, Lord knows the logs were many and we were so transfixed by Red Hen's live feed. Red Hen was a solider that ran hot now and then so being cold did him some good. Halfway toward the Hut he found the supply drop with each crate painted snow white.

He sat down and unloaded the contents of each carefully. Weapons and ammo and a microSD card to slide into his mind. We became concerned when he hung for a tad too long studying it but worse hiccups have happened before.

What I saw and read in the logs and have thought about as I am sure Red Hen thinks about is, well, something I'll always remember and hope never to forget. Red Hen's upload remembers its truth so perhaps ask \textit{it} one day. The operators recall wanting to see Red Hen's breath or wishing there was some mechanism that could be employed to allow for the release of vapor absurd as it was. It was an \textit{Owl Moon} night taken directly from a page of that children's book. It was as if Red Hen had eyes, I swear, and as he tilted his sensor head slightly toward the sky he just caught the General Atomics MQ-1 Predator moving at a pace of leisure across the silver moon. It was at this point we lost any real remote control we had over it. We hadn't any real idea how near or far the MQ-1 truly was as we were in a state of shock since seeing an MQ-1 flying at all and after so many years\dots and we knew it wasn't ours or hadn't been for a long time. This was Occupied Territory of the United States and had been for as long as any of us operators could remember. Red Hen judged it wasn't in too much danger of the drone or its Zuni rocket that had been modified and attached (we learned all about it much later) and decided to open its sensor head and scan the fax it had been handling. It wasn't any surprise why the designers had added this screen to Red Hen's head to be thrust open and closed as that was the Hollywood slop they were taught on. In any case, it allowed us to actually scan the fax for upload and to glean its physical proprieties; truly a marvelous thing. Red Hen had enough time for this and kept watch on the MQ-1 as it sailed in the chilly night sea above in a holding pattern we couldn't comprehend! We all watched it over and over again. It was as if Red Hen were framing us the shots of a movie so serene. Its onboard cameras had never recorded anything like it. Network was abuzz. Why had Red Hen waited to physically scan the fax now? We didn't know but surely all understand we were doing our best at the time to report it via Network and even hard line. We learned later that Wider Network and Red Hen had been pinging and we surmised whatever pinged Red Hen pinged him for this. Red Hen's thinking (at least then) was always available to us but it was moving like an old fashioned ticker tape we ignored being so consumed by our own screens and monitors. His thinking was right there and hadn't been scrambled. Looking back at that tape or scroll of thought we saw that Red Hen knew there was a page missing from the fax so he sucked back in his scan screen and shut his sensor head and made his way back toward the cabin looking like MacArthur wading ashore.

The MQ-1 was monitoring it all. Red Hen showed no real concern about that although he had armed itself with the equipment we had thought would be needless so we sent such old gear. All of the guns had a snow white finish:

Before he had set out again for the cabin Red Hen studied the sightings of the M9 and pulled the slide back to see the bullet nestled in the chamber. He holstered it and checked both the M7 and the M10. He decided on the ``MAC-10'' and ditched the M7 in the snow. We didn't think twice about that since Red Hen was making its own decisions and it wanted page two of that fax. And some things are ``Need To Know'' we reminded ourselves. The MQ-1 kept pattern as Red Hen got closer to the cabin. 

I can say with great confidence after having reviewed the data that the steel plates of his sensor head protecting the scan screen were colder than the logs had ever recorded them being before. The moon was the only light and it illuminated the cabin eerily when it came within sight of the cameras. Nothing moved just as nothing moved before he had arrived earlier. Yet Red Hen studied the cabin for a long time and there wasn't much thinking going on from what we could tell. We did notice, again in the logs and again after the fact, that its grip on the M10 was tightening beyond the standard programmed.

Red Hen, and by extension we remote operators, saw things we hadn't the first go around. Red Hen wanting to put a few rounds into his sensor head made more sense after what we all saw. The thought has been classified. By this time Network was informed enough to have dispatched another strike team. This Third Team was different in that human Advisors were accompanying the bots of Red Hen's make and model.

The First Team had done their job well and with no human Advisors present. This was an American first. Big American History if we're being totally honest and given this report is for the Network I wouldn't lie, I swear to God and our Lord and Savior Jesus Christ. The first team had been sent from the \textit{USS Jesus and Jenny} out in the Pacific. That just added to the historicity of it. Whereas Red Hen arrived via airdrop. Space Force handled Red Hen's insertion while the Navy was tasked with the first. And the Navy only started their flight when Red Hen was close enough; it had been hiking for a few days. Just the way it was. Navy boys said they'd get in and out. They pretty much did, I suppose but that MQ-1 had us wondering if they really had.

Red Hen, almost as if by inspiration, stood up from his prone position observing the cabin and walked slowly up to the front door. Nothing had been disturbed. The three sedans out front on the winding drive still contained their dead occupants. The First Team's bots had riddled the cars and we made a note about that. Seemed needless. Excessive. 

It wasn't a large cabin, there weren't many rooms. The windows had been blown away by gun fire and the pockmarks of bullets were everywhere. Broken glass and torn drapes; all of it sent back to Network. Red Hen did an odd thing and pushed open each door with the M10. Everything was so slow but Red Hen was framing it all like a movie for us. The First Team killed and searched but was ordered to take nothing. Perhaps they deliberately placed the first page of the fax on the desk in such disorder and decided to hide the other one under the corpse of the Chinese General's daughter.

Red Hen had checked every body, scanned every face. The death masks were 3-D printed after the bits and bytes made their way to Network. Ours and someone else's. This wasn't on mission and I can't recall a bot ever doing it. We wanted Red Hen to check on the MQ-1 and we issued orders to that effect but no pingback, no ack, yet the cameras of his recorded and sent back live to us all that it saw.

Red Hen turned over the little girl and hung for a long time. The Third Team's helicopter was inbound (all precaution had been tossed out the window) and Network was sure the Party was hijacking Red Hen via the MQ-1 but the Third Team, for whatever reason, wasn't told about it so when they hit the ground the MQ-1 fired off the Zuni and it's aim was true enough. It was a real shit show by then and Network had one line:

\begin{tabbing}
\= \qquad \= \qquad \= \qquad \= \\
\> \> \> \> \large \dots We say again: kill Red Hen.
\end{tabbing}

The Zuni had damaged but not disabled a bot with the moniker Gorman and killed an Advisor and the bird and the human flying it. The bot Vasquez and its Advisor were unharmed. They let the mangled Gorman take point.

Red Hen had been kneeling in the guts and blood of this kid and was ignoring the fax sheet papier-mâché on her body all splashed with blood, soggy with gore. It just had this little girl's face pressed against its scan screen with its sensor head exposed. Holding her tightly by the small of her back, arching her. That's what Gorman's dispatched mini drone recorded at least. Red Hen wasn't thinking logically as far as we could tell but we had become transfixed. All of Red Hen's thought were instantly classified. There was no way, then or now, to tell what it was thinking. It did have enough forethought to slide the microSD into its slot. All us remote operators were in an uproar as that microSD contained new mission parameters and we knew that the MQ-1 was getting it all in one form or another. To our immense relief we heard the whistle and snap of automatic fire.

Gorman instantly became an American hero to us. We were getting the action from many angels and with Red Hen becoming such a talented cinematographer it made for amusing viewing. Gorman's aim wasn't true, however. The Zuni had damaged enough to make Red Hen aware just in time to counter and avoid much damage. Red Hen was able to grab its ``MAC-10'' and squeeze off a few rounds that struck Gorman's standard issue rifle and tore into him at the right spots and perhaps even weakened his sensor head's eyes. We later discovered someone had stocked HV ammo for the M10 in the crate. Logs indicate it was most likely accidental. Gorman's reaction happened fast. Gorman rushed Red Hen, grabbed him by the neck and shoved him against the cabin's wall and began to punch at the speed of anime directly at Red Hen's torso motivated by the slightest sight of the black as motor oil blood that ran through the tubes that were their veins. Red Hen began his own punching focused on Gorman's left face plate or eye. He got many good licks and dented the damn thing to the point he was able to wedge his fingers underneath the corrugated wound and with his left hand drew his M9 and fired away at the innards of Gorman's ``skull''. 

Most of the vital workings of the bot aren't located there but enough matter and Gorman was damaged enough so that his backups weren't working as they should have been. It was at this point, as you know as well as I from the logs, that Red Hen stood up and the cameras were mostly offline so we didn't get a great glimpse. Vasquez and her Advisor were unloading everything heavy they had. Red Hen was undoubtedly being splintered apart with bullet hit squibs bursting. The footage from Vasquez did show him in a very Hollywood position of death: on his knees in a pool of his own bot guts and black engine oil. Almost Christlike? No! Thích Quảng Đức-like by God! The Advisor gathered up the microSD and faithfully crushed it.

Vasquez looked down at Red Hen and hung for a long time. The Advisor was scrambling for extract and was getting confused with each passing order not acknowledged. Vasquez looked up at a hole blown out of the cabin's ceiling from God knows what munition or perhaps it had just been torn off by all the rounds they had let off. She saw the MQ-1 and she saw the moon behind it and it was as if a lamp within glass stashed away in a nook suddenly became lit all within the reflection of her sensor head plate. She raised her sidearm and pointed it at her Advisor and her aim was true.\\
